Twin Cities Orthopedics ACL Knee Study

Paired-sample t-test and ARIMA time series analysis of 20 cadaveric knees to compare ACL reconstruction techniques. Findings presented to Twin Cities Orthopedics.

R Java Excel ARIMA Statistical Analysis

Overview

A significant subset of ACL reconstructions (ACLR) result in re-rupture. Two augmentation techniques — Modified Lemaire Lateral Extra-Articular Tenodesis (ML LET) and Anatomic Anterolateral Ligament Reconstruction (ALL) — may reduce this risk by improving rotational stability. This study asked three core questions:

  • Does an isolated ACLR restore full knee stability?
  • If not, does ML LET or ALL augmentation help?
  • Are there measurable mechanical differences between ML LET and ALL?

Tools & Technologies

R Java Excel Paired Sample T-Tests ARIMA Bonferroni Correction ADF Test KPSS Test

Dataset

The dataset came from biomechanical testing performed on 20 paired cadaveric knees (10 left, 10 right) at Twin Cities Orthopedics. Each knee was tested across 4 surgical states: Intact ACL, ACL Deficient, ACLR, and ACLR + either ML LET or ALL. Under each state, a robotic testing machine performed 11 standardized mechanical tests — including Internal Rotation (5 Nm torque, 0–90° flexion), Anterior Tibial Load (88 N force), and Simulated Pivot Shift (5 Nm + 10 Nm valgus torque). Data was recorded at 1 ms intervals, capturing 6 directional and rotational measurements per millisecond.

Process

  • Cleaned raw Excel data by subtracting equilibrium from the Intact state baseline, then normalizing all other states against that corrected Intact baseline.
  • Computed mean differences and standard deviations for all combinations of surgical state × trajectory × rotation.
  • Ran 44 paired sample t-tests (one per unique trajectory/rotation pair) comparing each reconstructed state against the intact knee.
  • Applied a Bonferroni correction (α = 0.05 ÷ 44 = 0.001136) to control for multiple comparisons.
  • Conducted an ARIMA time series analysis on a subset of 4 knees at Pivot 30° flexion, training each model on 903 ms of data and forecasting 100 ms forward.
  • Validated ARIMA stationarity using Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) and KPSS tests.

  • Posterior rotation showed the most instability — 17 statistically significant differences from intact across surgical states.
  • Internal rotation showed only 2 significant differences, and Valgus/Medial/Superior rotations showed just 1 combined.
  • ACLR + ALL produced the lowest average deviation from the intact knee (≈1.4°), outperforming both ACLR alone (≈1.75°) and ACLR + ML LET (≈2.5°).
  • ARIMA stationarity testing: ACLR and ALL were stationary (p = 0.01), while ML LET failed to achieve stationarity (p = 0.3135) — confirmed by KPSS (p = 0.01).
  • ALL showed a narrower ARIMA forecast band, indicating more consistent and predictable knee behavior over time.

Business Insights

These findings give surgeons a data-backed framework for choosing between augmentation techniques. Patients prioritizing knee stability may benefit more from ACLR + ML LET; patients prioritizing range of motion and mobility are better served by ACLR + ALL, which most closely matched the intact knee's behavior. The ARIMA results further suggest ALL produces more predictable long-term kinematics — a meaningful consideration for athletic populations at high re-rupture risk.
